Project HUMAN

Project Overview

It was my first year at the University of Virginia, and I had been looking forward to getting to meet more people from the computer science department. That was when I came accross a flyer that stated that read "HUMAN" across the front. I decided to look further into what the project was about. That was how I learned that Panagiotis Apostolellis from the CS department was looking for students to help researchs ways that we could use technology to bring people together. This seemed like the perfect opportunity not only to get involved in research, but to also use technology in a way to directly apply it to my own situation in a new environment.

Description

Our first meeting invitation came out near the end of September. I don’t believe any of us knew for sure what we had signed up for. I packed my gray backpack with my laptop, notebooks, pencils, and waterbottle, and waited at Rice Hall for our ride to come. Shortly after my arrival, a girl in a black Honda pulled up to the side of the building, and I timidly asked as a freshman might before heading into a random vehicle for a completely ambiguous event whether she was the driver for Project HUMAN.

After about 20 minutes in the car, we pulled up to a grassy area with makeshift soccer goals, and a large picnic blanket with Greek pastries sprawled across. The professor came over and introduced himself. In total, there were about 10 other students ranging from first years like myself to grad students who were there to learn more about this mystery project.

We started off the session with some team building exercises where we had to work in groups of 2 or 3 to act out scenarios, tell each other stories, and compete in games such as soccer. It was an untraditional but rewarding experience.

At the end of the semester, the professor invited all of the participants to stay over at a retreat on a lake to discuss our takeaways from the past few months. One the first day, he led us down a dirt path to a room carved into the side of a hill. This was a tech-free day where we were left to journal and illustrate our own belief of our values, individual stories, and what made us who we are.

Back at the retreat, the professor prompted each of us to come up with our own design for a type of technology that would help to build the same kind of connection for others that our experiences over the course of the project had done for us. This led to the design of an artifact that would later be known as Anthropo, a plush animal with a microphone embedded in one eye which allows for those holding it to record themselves talking about their day, stories, and experiences. The artifact could then be mailed to different people from around the world allowing their stories to spread without the necessity for digital devices.
